Poster Photography & the X-Rite ColorChecker Passport

by Sean Wallitsch - Nov 6th, 2014 - posted in color, photography, posters

A quick look at a color accurate photography for screen printed posters, and a review of the X-rite ColorChecker passport.

One of my hobbies is collecting limited edition screen printed posters, things like Spirited Away by Olly Moss or Don’t Walk (from Blade Runner) by Chris Thornley. Since another one of my hobbies is photography, it was only a matter of time before I began to go overboard combining the two.

A site called ExpressoBeans.com acts as a database for these prints, and let’s users submit additional photography for any prints, so that other users can get a better idea of how the print looks like in person.

Despite all these additional photos, collectors frequently run into two problems:

  1. The print, once actually screen printed, always looks slightly different from the official preview image released by the artist or gallery.
  2. Detail photographs taken by hobbyists always end up with varied colors from each other, and are rarely true to the actual print.

I decided that I would strive to provide the best, most color accurate, photos of all the screen prints in my collection.

Photography Setup

Photographers of prints usually run into several issues arising from the nature of the prints themselves.

  • Prints that were shipped rolled up can take weeks to flatten 98% of the way, and even months later might still have the remaining 2% at their very tips.
  • Without framing the prints, it’s difficult to stand a screen print vertically. Frequently screen prints are photographed flat on a table, floor, bed or other clear surface.

To solve these problems, I took a 32x40 acid-free foamboard and an extra mylar poster bag. Cutting up the poster bag, I was left with several 3 inch strips of mylar that I wrapped around the top and bottom of the foamboard. Prints could now be slipped under the mylar straps to keep prints flat during photography.
